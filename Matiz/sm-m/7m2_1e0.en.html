Battery Load Test

  1. Check the battery for obvious damage, such as a cracked or broken case or cover, which could permit the loss of electrolyte. If obvious damage is noted, replace the battery.

Caution : Do not charge the battery if the hydrometer is clear or light yellow. Instead, replace the battery. If the battery feels hot or if violent gassing or spewing of electrolyte through the vent hole occurs, discontinue charging or reduce the charging rate to avoid injury.

  1. Check the hydrometer. If the green dot is visible, go to the load test procedure. If the indicator is dark but green is not visible, charge the battery. For charging a battery removed from the vehicle, refer to "Charging a Completely Discharged Battery" in this section.
  2. Connect a voltmeter and a battery load tester across the battery terminals.
  3. Apply a 300-ampere load for 15 seconds to remove any surface charge from the battery.
  4. Remove the load.
  5. Wait 15 seconds to let the battery recover, and apply a 270-ampere load.

Important : The battery temperature must be estimated by touch and by the temperature condition the battery has been exposed to for the preceding few hours.

  1. If the voltage does not drop below the minimum listed, the battery is good and should be reinstalled. If the voltage is less than the minimum listed, replace the battery. Refer to "Battery Specifications" in this section.

Alternator Output Test

  1. Perform the alternator system test. Refer to "Alternator System Check" in this section.
  2. Replace the alternator if it fails that test. Refer to "Alternator" in the On-Vehicle Service section. If it passes the test, perform the on-vehicle output check which follows.

Important : Always check the alternator for output before assuming that a grounded "L" terminal circuit has damaged the regulator.

  1. Attach a digital multimeter (a), an ammeter (b), and a carbon pile load (c) to the battery (d) and the alternator (e) of the vehicle.



Important : Be sure the vehicle battery is fully charged, and the carbon pile load is turned off.

  1. With the ignition switch in the OFF position, check and record the battery voltage.
  2. Remove the harness connector from the generator.
  3. Turn the ignition switch to the ON position with the engine not running. Use a digital multimeter to check for voltage in the harness connector "L" terminal.
  4. The reading should be near the specified battery voltage of 12 volts. If the voltage is too low, check the indicator "L" terminal circuits for open and grounded circuits causing voltage loss. Correct any open wires, terminal connections, etc., as necessary. Refer to "Charging System" in this section.
  5. Attach the generator harness connector.
  6. Run the engine at a moderate idle, and measure the voltage across the battery terminals. The reading should be above that recorded in Step 4 but less than 15 volts. If the reading is over 15 volts or below the previous reading, replace the alternator. Refer to "Alternator" in the On-Vehicle Service section.
  7. Run the engine at a moderate idle, and measure the alternator amperage output.
  8. Turn on the carbon pile, and adjust it to obtain the maximum amps while maintaining the battery voltage above 13 volts.
  9. If the reading is within 15 amps of the alternator's rating noted on the alternator, the generator is good. If not, replace the alternator. Refer to "Alternator" in the On-Vehicle Service section.
  10. With the alternator operating at the maximum output, measure the voltage between the alternator housing and the battery negative terminal. The voltage drop should be 0.5 volt or less. If the voltage drop is more than 0.5 volt, check the ground path from the alternator housing to the negative battery cable.
  11. Check, clean, tighten, and recheck all of the ground connections.

Alternator System Check

When operating normally, the alternator indicator lamp will come on when the ignition switch is in the ON position and go out when the engine starts. If the lamp operates abnormally or if an undercharged or overcharged battery condition occurs, the following procedure may be used to diagnose the charging system. Remember that an undercharged battery is often caused by accessories being left on overnight or by a defective switch that allows a lamp, such as a trunk or glove box lamp, to stay on.
Diagnose the generator with the following procedure:
  1. Visually check the belt and wiring.
  2. With the ignition switch in the ON position and the engine stopped, the charge indicator lamp should be on. If not, detach the harness at the alternator and ground the "L" terminal in the harness with a fused, 5-ampere jumper lead.
    • If the lamp lights, replace the alternator. Refer to "Alternator" in the On-Vehicle Service section.
    • If the lamp does not light, locate the open circuit between the ignition switch and the harness connector. The indicator lamp bulb may be burned out.
  3. With the ignition switch in the ON position and the engine running at moderate speed, the charge indicator lamp should be off. If not, detach the wiring harness at the generator.
    • If the lamp goes off, replace the alternator. Refer to "Alternator" in the On-Vehicle Service section.
    • If the lamp stays on, check for a short to ground in the harness between the connector and the indicator lamp.

Important : Always check the alternator for output before assuming that a grounded "L" terminal circuit has damaged the regulator. Refer to "Alternator" in the Unit Repair section.
